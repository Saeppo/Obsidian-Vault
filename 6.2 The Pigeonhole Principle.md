Each term is smaller than the one before it (e.g., $9, 6, 3$).

Let's define a pair of numbers for every term $a_k$ in the sequence:

- $i_k$: The length of the longest **increasing** subsequence that _ends_ at $a_k$.
    
- $d_k$: The length of the longest **decreasing** subsequence that _ends_ at $a_k$.
    
Every term $a_k$ in the sequence is an **object** ($N=n^2+1$). The boxes are the possible pairs of lengths $(i_k, d_k)$.

## üìù Combinatorial Rules Review

These rules govern basic counting operations1111.

### ‚ûï Sum and Subtraction Rules (Slide 2)

- The Sum Rule: For disjoint (mutually exclusive) finite sets $S_{1},S_{2},...,S_{n}$ where $S_{i}\cap S_{j}=\emptyset$ for any $i\ne j$2, the size of their union is the sum of their sizes:
    
    $$|\bigcup_{i=1}^{n}S_{i}|=\sum_{i=1}^{n}|S_{i}|$$
- The Subtraction Rule (Inclusion-Exclusion for two sets): For any finite sets $S_{1}$ and $S_{2}$, the size of their union is:
    
    $$|S_{1}\cup S_{2}|=|S_{1}|+|S_{2}|-|S_{1}\cap S_{2}|$$
### ‚úñÔ∏è Product and Division Rules (Slide 3)

- The Product Rule: For any finite sets $S_{1},S_{2},...,S_{n}$5, the number of elements in their Cartesian product (sequences of choices) is the product of their sizes:
    
    $$|S_{1}\times S_{2}\times\cdot\cdot\cdot\times S_{n}|=|S_{1}|\cdot|S_{2}|\cdot...\cdot|S_{n}|$$
- The Division Rule: If a finite set $A$ is partitioned into $n$ disjoint subsets $B_1, \dots, B_n$ (i.e., $A=\bigcup B_i$) 7, and every subset has the same size $|B_i|=d$8, then the number of subsets $n$ is:
    
    $$n = |A|/d$$

## üïäÔ∏è The Pigeonhole Principles

These principles guarantee the _existence_ of a certain outcome if the number of objects exceeds the number of categories.

### 1. The Pigeonhole Principle (PCP) (Slides 11, 20)

- **Definition (Theorem 6.2.1):** Let $k\ge1$ be an integer. When $k+1$ or more objects ($N \ge k+1$) are placed into $k$ boxes, there is at least one box that contains at least two of the objects10101010.
    
- **Corollary 6.3.1 (Function Mapping):** A function $f:M\rightarrow N$ where the domain $|M|$ is an integer larger than the codomain $|N|$ is **not one-to-one** (injective)11.
    

### 2. The Generalized Pigeonhole Principle (GPP) (Slides 17, 20)

- **Definition (Theorem 6.2.2):** Let $N, k\ge1$ be integers. When $N$ or more objects are placed into $k$ boxes, there exists at least one box that contains a minimum of $m$ objects12.
    
- Minimum Guaranteed Occupancy ($m$): The number of objects guaranteed in at least one box is:
    
    $$m = \left\lceil \frac{N}{k} \right\rceil$$
    
    13
    
    - _Where:_ $N$ = objects (pigeons), $k$ = boxes (pigeonholes).
        
- Minimum Required Objects ($N_{min}$): To guarantee at least $m$ objects in one of the $k$ boxes, you need:
    
    $$N_{min} = k \cdot (m - 1) + 1$$
